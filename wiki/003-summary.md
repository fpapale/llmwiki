**Signal Processing for Telecommunications and Economics Lab – AI & Machine Learning Overview**  

| **Topic** | **Key Points** |
|---|---|
| **Artificial Intelligence (AI)** | • Discipline of computer science that designs hardware/software capable of human‑like performance. <br>• *Effetto IA*: once we understand how a machine works, we stop seeing it as intelligent. |
| **AI Taxonomy** | • **ANI (Artificial Narrow Intelligence)** – current “weak” AI, task‑specific (e.g., smartphones, big‑data analysis). <br>• **AGI (Artificial General Intelligence)** – future “strong” AI, capable of human‑level perception, language, reasoning. <br>• **ASI (Artificial Superintelligence)** – theoretical stage where AI surpasses all human and artificial cognition. |
| **ChatGPT** | Classified as **ANI**: excels at natural‑language tasks but lacks general intelligence. |
| **Anthropomorphic Bias** | Humans tend to attribute human traits to AI, leading to misconceptions about AI’s capabilities and moral agency. |
| **Ethical & Legal Issues** | • Freedom, moral responsibility, and legal liability of AI systems are still open questions. <br>• Debate on “robotic culpability” and appropriate sanctions. |
| **AI System Types** | 1. **Rule‑based (classical)** – IF‑THEN logic; limited flexibility. <br>2. **Expert Systems** – rely on domain experts; high rule memory, costly to build. <br>3. **Machine Learning (ML)** – data‑driven, agnostic to domain; advantages: no expert needed, can capture unconscious patterns; drawbacks: black‑box, data‑quality dependent. |
| **Bias in AI** | • *Automation bias* can reproduce discrimination (race, gender, age). <br>• Real‑world examples: OPTUM (healthcare), Amazon recruiting tool, COMPAS (recidivism), facial‑recognition APIs. |
| **Mitigation Strategies** | • Use representative, high‑quality data. <br>• Design fair, inclusive algorithms. <br>• Hybrid human‑AI decision making. <br>• **Explainable AI (XAI)** – provide transparent reasoning, confidence levels, error analysis. |
| **Social Risks & Opportunities** | **Risks**: diffuse responsibility (e.g., 2010 flash crash), ethical indifference, malicious misuse (weaponized drones, disease‑spreading algorithms). <br>**Opportunities**: improved healthcare diagnostics, smart cities (energy, mobility, disaster detection), education for future AI‑centric jobs. |
| **Learning Paradigms** | • **Supervised** – labeled input/output pairs (classification, regression). <br>• **Unsupervised** – discover structure (clustering, dimensionality reduction). <br>• **Reinforcement** – reward/punishment feedback (games, control). |
| **Core ML Models** | • *Supervised*: SVM, decision trees, neural nets, Naïve Bayes. <br>• *Unsupervised*: K‑means, hidden Markov models, SOMs. |
| **Neural Networks** | • Inspired by biological neurons; consist of layers (input, hidden, output). <br>• Knowledge stored in connection weights; learned via training algorithms. <br>• Key concepts: activation functions, perceptron, feed‑forward architecture, expressive capacity. |

*Overall, the material outlines the foundations of AI, distinguishes between narrow, general, and super intelligence, highlights ethical challenges (bias, responsibility), and presents machine‑learning fundamentals—especially neural networks—as the driving technology for current and future AI applications.*
